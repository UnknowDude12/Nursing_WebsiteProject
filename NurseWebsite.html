Nurse History

Since in the mid-19th centurythe history of professional nursing traditionally begins with Florence Nightingale. Nightingale, the well-educated daughter of wealthy British parents, defied social conventions and decided to become a nurse. The nursing of strangers, either in hospitals or in their homes, was not then seen as a respectable career for well-bred ladies, who, if they wished to nurse, were expected to do so only for sick family and intimate friends. In a radical departure from these views, Nightingale believed that well-educated women, using scientific principles and informed education about healthy lifestyles, could dramatically improve the care of sick patients. Moreover, she believed that nursing provided an ideal independent calling full of intellectual and social freedom for women, who at that time had few other career options.

Medical Abbreviations

Complete Blood Count (CBC)

A Complete Blood Count (CBC) is a standard blood test that evaluates the quantity and quality of different types of cells in your blood. It includes red blood cells, which carry oxygen, white blood cells, which fight infections, and platelets, which help with clotting. This test is a common tool used by doctors to assess overall health, identify a wide range of disorders, and monitor the health status of individuals with known conditions affecting blood cells.

Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG)

An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a non-invasive medical test that records the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time using electrodes placed on the skin. This test is instrumental in diagnosing a variety of heart conditions. For instance, it can detect arrhythmias, which are irregular heartbeats, and identify signs of a heart attack by revealing patterns indicating myocardial infarction. Additionally, an ECG can diagnose heart failure by showing abnormalities in heart rhythm or structural issues.

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) is a test that measures the amount of urea nitrogen, a waste product, in your blood. Urea is formed in the liver as an end product of protein metabolism and is carried in the blood to the kidneys for elimination. The BUN test is primarily used, along with the creatinine test, to evaluate kidney function in a wide range of circumstances, to help diagnose kidney disease, and to monitor people with acute or chronic kidney dysfunction or failure.

Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP)

The Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) is a blood test that provides important information about your body's metabolism. It measures the glucose level, electrolyte and fluid balance, and kidney function. Specifically, it includes tests for blood glucose, calcium, electrolytes (sodium, potassium, carbon dioxide, and chloride), and kidney tests (BUN and creatinine). These tests can help diagnose and monitor conditions such as diabetes, kidney disease, and respiratory problems.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive medical imaging technique that uses a powerful magnetic field and computer-generated radio waves to create detailed images of the organs and tissues in your body. MRI can be used to diagnose a variety of conditions, from torn ligaments and tumors to stroke and multiple sclerosis. It can also be used to monitor disease progression and response to treatment.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET)

Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a type of nuclear imaging test that helps doctors see how the organs and tissues inside your body are functioning. It uses a small amount of a radioactive drug, or tracer, which is absorbed by the particular organ or tissue being examined. PET scans are often used to diagnose conditions, plan treatment, check whether a disease is spreading, or monitor the effectiveness of a treatment.

Computed Tomography (CT)

Computed Tomography (CT) is a medical imaging technique that uses X-ray measurements taken from different angles and computer processing to create cross-sectional images of the body. CT scans provide more detailed information than regular X-rays and are used to diagnose conditions such as cancer, cardiovascular disease, infectious disease, trauma, and musculoskeletal disorders.

X-ray

An X-ray is a quick and painless imaging test that produces images of the structures inside your body, particularly your bones. X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation and are used to detect fractures, infections, arthritis, and other abnormalities in bones and joints. They can also be used to diagnose conditions affecting soft tissues, such as pneumonia or breast cancer.

Ultrasound (US)

Ultrasound, also known as sonography, is a medical imaging technique that uses high-frequency sound waves to produce images of structures within the body. It is commonly used to monitor pregnancy, diagnose the causes of pain, swelling, and infection in the body’s internal organs, and to guide procedures such as needle biopsies. Unlike X-rays, ultrasound does not use ionizing radiation.

Hemoglobin A1C (HbA1c)

Hemoglobin A1C (HbA1c) is a blood test that measures your average blood sugar levels over the past three months. It is commonly used to diagnose and monitor diabetes. The test reflects the percentage of hemoglobin molecules in your blood that have glucose attached to them. Higher A1C levels indicate poorer blood sugar control and a higher risk of diabetes complications.

Liver Function Tests (LFTs)

Liver Function Tests (LFTs) are a group of blood tests that assess the health of your liver by measuring levels of proteins, liver enzymes, and bilirubin in your blood. These tests help diagnose liver diseases, monitor the progression of liver conditions, and evaluate the effectiveness of treatment. Common LFTs include ALT, AST, ALP, and bilirubin.

C-Reactive Protein (CRP)

C-Reactive Protein (CRP) is a blood test marker for inflammation in the body. Elevated CRP levels can indicate an acute infection, chronic inflammatory disease, or other conditions. It is commonly used to assess the risk of cardiovascular disease and to monitor the effectiveness of treatment for inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis and lupus.

Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH)

Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) is a blood test that measures the amount of TSH in your blood. TSH is produced by the pituitary gland and regulates the production of thyroid hormones. This test is used to diagnose and monitor thyroid disorders such as hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism. Abnormal TSH levels can indicate issues with the thyroid gland.

Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA)

Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) is a blood test used primarily to screen for prostate cancer. The test measures the amount of PSA, a protein produced by the prostate gland, in the blood. Elevated PSA levels can indicate prostate cancer, benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), or prostatitis. It is also used to monitor the effectiveness of treatment for prostate cancer.

Lipid Panel

A Lipid Panel is a blood test that measures the levels of specific lipids in your blood, including total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol (bad cholesterol), HDL cholesterol (good cholesterol), and triglycerides. This test helps assess your risk of cardiovascular disease and guides the management of conditions such as hyperlipidemia and atherosclerosis.

Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)

A Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP) is a blood test that provides information about your body’s metabolism. It measures glucose, calcium, proteins, electrolytes, kidney function, and liver function. This test helps diagnose and monitor conditions such as diabetes, liver disease, and kidney disease.

Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG)

Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG) is a hormone produced during pregnancy. A blood or urine test measures the level of hCG to confirm pregnancy. In addition to pregnancy testing, hCG levels can be used to monitor the health of the pregnancy, diagnose ectopic pregnancies, and as part of screening for certain cancers.

Prothrombin Time and International Normalized Ratio (PT/INR)

Prothrombin Time (PT) and International Normalized Ratio (INR) are blood tests that measure how long it takes for blood to clot. They are used to monitor individuals taking blood-thinning medications (anticoagulants) and to diagnose bleeding disorders. PT/INR tests help ensure that patients receive the correct dosage of anticoagulant medications and maintain the right balance between preventing clots and causing excessive bleeding.

Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)

Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) is a blood test that measures how quickly red blood cells settle at the bottom of a test tube. An elevated ESR indicates inflammation in the body and is used to diagnose and monitor conditions such as infections, autoimmune diseases, and cancers. ESR is a non-specific test and is usually used in conjunction with other tests to diagnose specific conditions.

Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)

Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs) are a group of tests that measure how well your lungs are working. These tests evaluate lung volume, capacity, rates of flow, and gas exchange. They are used to diagnose and monitor lung diseases such as asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and other conditions that affect breathing.

Common Medications

Acetaminophen

Acetaminophen is used to treat mild to moderate pain and reduce fever. It is often found in over-the-counter pain relievers and fever reducers.

Ibuprofen

Ibuprofen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used to relieve pain, reduce inflammation, and lower fever. Common brand names include Advil and Motrin.

Lisinopril

Lisinopril is an ACE inhibitor used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension) and heart failure. It can also improve survival after a heart attack.

Atorvastatin

Atorvastatin is a statin medication used to lower cholesterol and triglyceride levels in the blood. It can also reduce the risk of heart attack and stroke.

Metformin

Metformin is an oral diabetes medication that helps control blood sugar levels. It is commonly used to treat type 2 diabetes.

Omeprazole

Omeprazole is a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) that reduces stomach acid production. It is used to treat gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) and other stomach acid-related conditions.

Losartan

Losartan is an angiotensin II receptor blocker (ARB) used to treat high blood pressure and protect the kidneys from damage due to diabetes.

Amoxicillin

Amoxicillin is a penicillin antibiotic used to treat a variety of bacterial infections. It works by stopping the growth of bacteria.

Clopidogrel

Clopidogrel is an antiplatelet medication used to prevent heart attacks and strokes in individuals with heart disease. It works by preventing blood clots.

Furosemide

Furosemide is a diuretic (water pill) used to treat fluid retention (edema) and swelling caused by heart failure, liver disease, or kidney disease.

Warfarin

Warfarin is an anticoagulant (blood thinner) used to prevent blood clots in veins and arteries, reducing the risk of stroke, heart attack, and other serious conditions.

Prednisone

Prednisone is a corticosteroid used to treat inflammation and immune system disorders. It is used for conditions such as allergies, asthma, and arthritis.

Sertraline

Sertraline is an antidepressant (SSRI) used to treat depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, and OCD. It works by increasing levels of serotonin in the brain.

Escitalopram

Escitalopram is an SSRI antidepressant used to treat major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder. It helps restore the balance of serotonin in the brain.

Ranitidine

Ranitidine is an H2 blocker used to reduce stomach acid. It is commonly used to treat and prevent ulcers in the stomach and intestines and to treat GERD.

Tamsulosin

Tamsulosin is an alpha-blocker used to treat symptoms of an enlarged prostate (BPH). It helps to relax the muscles in the prostate and bladder neck, making it easier to urinate.

Citalopram

Citalopram is an SSRI antidepressant used to treat depression. It works by helping to restore the balance of serotonin in the brain.

Fluoxetine

Fluoxetine is an SSRI antidepressant used to treat depression, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), bulimia nervosa, and panic disorder. It helps to restore the balance of serotonin in the brain.
